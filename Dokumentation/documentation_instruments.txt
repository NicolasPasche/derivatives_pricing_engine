/*
    Function: zero_coupon_bond

    Purpose:
    -------
        Computes the price of a Zero-Coupon bond.
    
    A Zero-Coupon bond is a very simple financial instrument:

        - It pays 1 unit of money
        - At a future time T
        - It does not pay any interest (coupon) before maturity

    Therefore:

        The question is:
        What is 1 unit of money worth today, if it will only be received in the future?

    Mathematical formula:

        Z = e⁻ʳᵀ

    Interpretation of variables:

        double r = risk-free interest rate
        double T = time to maturity (in years)

    Conceptual interpretation:
    -------------------------

    exp(-r * T) is called a discount factor. It reduces the future payment (1) to its present value.
    The higher the interest rate (r) or the longer the time to maturity (T), the smaller the present value of the future payment

    Why this matters:
    
    Discounting future payments is a fundamental idea in finance.

    In fact:

        Every Balck-Scholes formula uses discounting to compute the present value of expected future payoffs.

    Therefore:

        Zero-Coupon bonds are building blocks for option pricing.
    */


/*
    Function: forward_price

    Purpose:
    --------
        Computes the price of a Forward Contract.

    A Forward Contract is an agreement to:

     - Buy (or sell) an asset at a fixed price at future time T

    Mathematical formula:
    
        F = S * e⁽ʳ⁻ᵈ⁾ᵀ

    Interpretation of parameters:
    
        double S = current stock price (spot price)
        double r = risk-free interest rate
        double d = dividend yield (represents payments recieved while holding the stock)
        double T = time to maturity of the forward contract (in years)

    Conceptual interpretation:
    -------------------------

    The forward price represents the fair future value of the stock.
    
    The stock price grows over time due to interest:

        eʳᵀ
    
    However:

        Dividends reduce the effective groth rate of the stock.

    Therefore:
    
        groth rate = r - d
        
    Final interpretation:

        Forward price = Current price grown at rate (r - d) over time T

    Why this matters:

    Forward prices are extremely important in financial mathematics. Many option formulas, including the Black-Scholes formula, 
    are often expressed in terms of the forward price rather than the spot price. This is because the forward price already 
    incorporates the cost of carrying the underlying asset, making it a more natural variable for pricing derivatives.

     */


/*
    Function: call_price

    Purpose:
    ---------
    Computes the theoretical price of a European call option using the Black-Scholes formula.

    Black-Scholes formula for a European call option:

        C = S * e⁻ᵈᵀ * N(d1) - K * e⁻ʳᵀ * N(d2)

    Interpretation:

    First term:
        S * e⁻ᵈᵀ * N(d1)

        Represents the expected value of the stock if the optoin finishes in-the-money.
        
        e⁻ᵈᵀ accounts for the present value of the expected stock price, considering the dividend yield.

    Second term:
        K * e⁻ʳᵀ * N(d2)

        Represents the discounted value of the strike that must be paid if the option is exercised.

    Final result:
        Call price = Expected stock value - Expected strike payment
        */


/*
    Function: put_price
    
    Purpose:
    --------
    Computes the theoretical price of a European put option using the Black-Scholes formula.

    Black-Scholes formula for a European put option:

        P = K * e⁻ʳᵀ * N(-d2) - S * e⁻ᵈᵀ * N(-d1)

    Interpretation:

    First term:
        K * e⁻ʳᵀ * N(-d2)

        Represents the discounted value of the strike that will be received if the option is exercised.

    Second term:
        S * e⁻ᵈᵀ * N(-d1)

        Represents the expected value of the stock if the option finishes in-the-money.

    Final result:
        Put price = Expected strike payment - Expected stock value
*/
