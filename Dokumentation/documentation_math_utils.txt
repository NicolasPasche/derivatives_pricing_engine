/*
    The C++ standard library does not provide a direct normal CDF function. 
    However, we can compute it using the complementary error function:

        N(x) = 0.5 * erfc(-x / sqrt(2))

    Parameters:
    ----------
    x : double
        Value at which the CDF is evaluated.

    Returns:
    ----------
    double
        Probability that a standard normal random variable is less than or equal to x.
*/



/*
    Function: d1

    Purpose:
    ---------

        Computes the quantity d1 used in the Black-Scholes option pricing formula.

    Mathematical definition:

        d1 = [ln(S/K) + (r - d + 0.5 * sigma^2) * T]
            -----------------------------------------
                        sigma * sqrt(T)

    Interpretation of parameters:
    ----------------------------

        double S = current stock price (spot price)
        double K = strike price of the option
        double r = risk-free interest rate
        double d = dividend yield of the underlying asset
        double sigma = volatility of the underlying asset
        double T = time to maturity of the option (in years)

    Conceptual interpretation:
    -------------------------
        d1 measures how far the stock price is from the strike price, 
        measured in units of volatility.

        In simpler terms:

        It tells us how likely it is that the option will finish in-the-money at expiration.

    Mathematical components explained:
    ---------------------------------

    ln (S/K): Measures how far the stock price is from the strike price on a logarithmic scale. 
              If S > K, this term is positive, indicating the option is currently in-the-money.
              If S < K, this term is negative, indicating the option is currently out-of-the-money.

    (r - d + 0.5*sigma²) * T: Represents the expected growth of the stock price over time, 
                              adjusted for dividends and volatility. 
                              The term (r - d) accounts for the net cost of carrying the stock 
                              (interest rate minus dividend yield), while 0.5*sigma² accounts for the 
                              variance of the stock price.
    */


/*
    Function: d2
    
    Purpose:
    --------

        Computes the quantity d2 used in the Black-Scholes formula.

    Mathematical definition:
    -----------------------

        d2 = d1 - sigma * sqrt(T)

    Interpretation of variables:
    ---------------------------

        double S = current stock price (spot price)
        double K = strike price
        double r = risk-free interest rate
        double d = dividend yield
        double sigma = volatility of the underlying asset
        double T = time to maturity of the option (in years)

    Conceptual interpretation:
    -------------------------

        d2 represents represents a probability-related measure that is slightly smaller than d1.
        While d1 is related to the expected position of the stock price, d2 accounts for the uncertainty (volatility) over time.

        In simpler terms:

        d1 measures expected future position
        d2 measures exercise probability
*/
